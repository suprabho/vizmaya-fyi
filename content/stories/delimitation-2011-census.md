# A New Political Map

If India's Parliament expands its lower house, the Lok Sabha, and reallocates seats based on the 2011 Census, the country's political geography would fundamentally change. 

New legislation proposes to increase the number of seats, unfreeze the seat allocation that has been pegged to the 1971 census for decades, and use the 2011 census to redraw the electoral map. This move would shift the balance of power, creating clear winners and losers among the states.

---

## 850

*Proposed seats in the Lok Sabha, up from 543*

The Constitution (131st Amendment) Bill and a companion Delimitation Bill seek to raise the Lok Sabha ceiling to 850 seats. The stated purpose is to enable the reservation of seats for women.

Crucially, the bills mandate that seat allocation follow the principle of population proportionality based on the latest available census—currently, the one from 2011. This breaks from a 50-year-old freeze designed to protect states that had successfully implemented family planning policies.

---

## Who Gains and Who Loses

While government ministers have suggested a “proportional increase” across the country, the proposed legislation contains no such guarantee. Article 81(2)(a) of the Constitution mandates allocation based on population, not a uniform percentage increase for every state.

Using the 2011 Census data to allocate 850 seats reveals a stark disparity compared to a scenario where every state's current share of seats is preserved.

---

## A Stark Divide

The five states gaining the most seats relative to a proportional increase are all in the Hindi Heartland. The five that lose the most are predominantly in the South and East.

---

## A New Political Arithmetic

This shift reflects decades of divergent demographic trends across the country, creating a new political arithmetic where population size becomes the primary determinant of parliamentary power.

The five-decade freeze on seat allocation—pegged to the 1971 Census—was designed precisely to prevent this outcome. Its removal recasts the rules of representation for every Indian state.

---

## The Hindi Heartland: Overwhelming Beneficiaries

States in northern India, with their higher population growth since 1971, would be the primary beneficiaries of the new allocation. 

Uttar Pradesh would gain 13 seats more than it would under a proportional increase. Bihar would gain 10, and Rajasthan 8. This concentration of gains would significantly amplify the region's influence in national politics.

---

## The South: A Sharp Erosion

Conversely, southern states that have led the nation in population control, education, and healthcare would see their share of parliamentary representation shrink significantly.

Tamil Nadu stands to lose 11 seats compared to a proportional increase, while Kerala would lose 8. These states are effectively penalized for their developmental successes, which resulted in slower population growth.

---

## A National Rebalancing

When aggregated by region, the pattern is unmistakable. The Hindi-Heartland's share of the 850-seat house would rise by five percentage points, from 38.1% to 43.1%.

The South’s share would fall by 3.6 points, from 24.3% to 20.7%. The North-East and Eastern regions would also see their influence decline. This represents the most significant shift in India's federal power balance in half a century.

---

## The Demographic Roots

The roots of this asymmetry lie in decades of differing fertility rates. The very reason the 1971 census freeze was enacted—to avoid penalizing states with successful family planning programs—is the same reason the proposed change is so consequential today.

The demographic gap between the North and South has narrowed, but it persists, and its political consequences are now set to be codified.

---

## A Tale of Two Fertility Rates

According to the National Family Health Survey (NFHS-5, 2019-21), all five southern states have Total Fertility Rates (TFR) between 1.5 and 1.8, well below the replacement level of 2.1.

Meanwhile, states like Bihar (3.0), Meghalaya (2.9), and Uttar Pradesh (2.4) remain at or above replacement level. This demographic divergence is the engine driving the reallocation of political power.

---

## Power vs. Progress

The data lays bare the core tension of the delimitation exercise. The constitutional mandate to allocate seats by population rewards demographic weight, not developmental achievement.

States that invested heavily in health, education, and family planning will see their share of parliamentary power shrink. The result is a political map that reflects population numbers from a decade ago, potentially at the expense of the very states that have been models of progress.

---

## Methodology & Sources

Data and analysis are based on the Census of India, 2011; the National Family Health Survey (NFHS-5, 2019-21); and the proposed Constitution (131st Amendment) Bill, 2026, and Delimitation Bill, 2026.

Seat gain/loss calculations by The Hindu Data Team compare a proportional allocation of 850 seats based on 2011 population against a uniform increase that preserves existing state-wise seat shares.
