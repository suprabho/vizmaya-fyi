# Mapped: AI Adoption Across Europe

*Eurostat's early-2026 digital-use release and IAB UK's parallel survey agree on the headline: AI use is rising across Europe, but not at the same speed. Norway has pulled almost three in five residents into recent use. Romania, Turkey, and Italy are still below one in five. Between them sits a continent splitting into tiers.*

**By vizmaya · April 2026**

---

## 56.3%

*Share of Norwegians who used an AI tool in the last three months, early 2026 — the highest rate in Europe*

Three in five. No other European country has yet crossed fifty percent. The number did not exist a year ago; Norway's 2025 figure was closer to forty.

Consumer AI, the charts are beginning to show, spreads the way broadband did — fastest in small, rich, digitally-literate countries first, and then unevenly.

---

## A continent splitting in tiers

Since ChatGPT's consumer release in late 2022, European AI adoption has begun to separate into clear tiers rather than move as a bloc.

At the top sit the Nordic and Baltic economies, together with a cluster of small, wealthy states. In the middle sit most of Western and Southern Europe. At the bottom — well below the EU average of 32.7 percent — sit Italy, Turkey, and much of the former communist bloc. The spread from top to bottom is now almost forty percentage points.

---

## Act I: The Nordic and Baltic lead

Seven of the top ten European countries by recent AI use are either Nordic or Baltic. Norway (56.3), Denmark (48.4), Estonia (46.6), Finland (46.3), Sweden (42.0), and Iceland-adjacent Ireland (44.9) all cluster well above the continental average.

The pattern is familiar to anyone who watched earlier digital waves. Small populations, high English proficiency, strong public digital infrastructure, and a long habit of installing consumer software early have, once again, delivered the first movers of a new computing wave.

---

## 47.0%

*Share of Swiss residents using AI tools in the last three months — the highest outside the Nordic–Baltic cluster*

Switzerland is the outlier of the top tier. Not Nordic, not Baltic, not particularly young — but sitting third on the continent, level with Estonia and ahead of Finland.

The Swiss figure is largely a story about income and installed base. The country has one of the highest rates of paid ChatGPT and Claude subscriptions in Europe, the densest concentration of AI-adjacent employers per capita, and the linguistic variety to make multilingual models immediately useful. The adoption curve is steepest where the willingness-to-pay curve already was.

---

## Act II: The Mediterranean split

Across Southern Europe, the map breaks cleanly in two.

Malta (46.5), Cyprus (44.2), and Greece (44.1) sit in the top ten. Italy (19.9) and Turkey (18.6) sit near the bottom. The gap is more than twenty-five percentage points between neighbours that share a sea.

The split does not line up with income, age structure, or even broadband penetration in any simple way. What it does line up with is English-language usage online, the share of the workforce in services rather than industry, and — tellingly — the degree to which consumer AI has been actively covered in national media.

---

## 46.5%

*Malta's AI usage rate — ahead of Finland, Ireland, and every large EU economy*

Malta is the surprise of the top tier. Small, English-speaking, service-heavy, and running one of Europe's most mature digital-services exports, it has taken to consumer AI faster than any country outside the Nordic core.

The Maltese figure also flatters the island's average age, which skews older than neighbouring tiers. The reading is not that young Maltese are unusually AI-fluent but that the island's workforce uses these tools as commonly as its Nordic peers — a notable result for a country that was, a decade ago, digitally unremarkable.

---

## Act III: The largest economies lag

The continent's biggest economies are not leading this wave. They are, mostly, being led by it.

Germany (32.3), France (37.5), and the United Kingdom (34.3) all sit below the middle of the European ranking. Spain (37.9) and Italy (19.9) are further behind. Together these five countries account for more than half the EU's population and a clear majority of its GDP — and yet they are collectively pulling the European average down, not up.

---

## 32.3%

*Share of German residents using AI tools in the last three months — below the EU average*

Germany is the most consequential laggard. Europe's largest economy, its industrial engine, and its largest internal market for software — and just under a third of its population reports recent AI use.

Part of the answer is structural. German data-protection culture, industrial-workforce composition, and a historically cautious corporate IT posture all slow consumer-AI adoption relative to Swedish or Dutch norms. Part is generational: German AI use among the over-45 age cohort is roughly half the Norwegian equivalent. The line will keep moving, but from behind.

---

## The youth divergence

Beneath every national number sits a sharper age gradient.

In the UK, overall recent AI use stands at 34 percent — but among 15-to-24-year-olds, 24 percent report using these tools *daily*. The equivalent daily-use figure for the over-55 cohort is in the low single digits.

Similar patterns show up everywhere the underlying microdata is published. National averages understate how embedded these tools already are among younger users, and overstate how embedded they are across the full adult population.

---

## 24%

*Share of UK 15–24-year-olds who use AI tools daily — roughly one in four*

One in four is the more arresting read of the UK data. It is not about access or familiarity; it is about routine. A quarter of the youngest adult cohort now uses generative AI tools every day, for schoolwork, for creative drafts, for search replacement, for coding.

That is a higher daily-use rate than the *overall recent-use* rate in a dozen European countries, including Italy, Turkey, Poland, Bulgaria, and Romania. The generational gap, in some places, is larger than the country-to-country gap at the top of the map.

---

## Act IV: The eastern edge of adoption

At the bottom of the European ranking sit countries that have spent the last decade closing earlier digital gaps — only to open a new one.

Poland (22.7), Bulgaria (22.5), North Macedonia (22.0), Bosnia & Herzegovina (20.3), and Romania (17.8) all sit below a quarter of the population reporting recent AI use. Hungary (29.6), Croatia (27.5), and Slovakia (30.8) sit just above.

The regional pattern is not mainly about infrastructure. Broadband penetration and smartphone use in this group are broadly comparable to Southern Europe. What differs is paid-service uptake, English-language media exposure, and the share of the workforce in AI-adjacent industries. These are the inputs to adoption, and they take longer to change than signup screens.

---

## What the map misses

Country-level choropleths flatten three things that matter.

The first is age: every country shown is a weighted average of a cohort that uses these tools daily and a cohort that barely does. The second is paid-versus-free use: the European paid-subscriber gradient is even steeper than the recent-use gradient. The third is work-versus-leisure: in Switzerland and Ireland, most recent AI use happens in paid professional work; in Greece and Cyprus, a larger share is leisure and study.

The national map is the cleanest read available right now. It is also, already, losing resolution on the thing it is trying to measure.

---

## Methodology & sources

Country-level AI usage figures are drawn from Eurostat's early-2026 release on individuals' use of AI tools in the preceding three months (isoc_ci_ai_i), with UK figures supplemented from IAB UK's *Attitudes to AI 2026* survey to match the Eurostat definition. Figures for Norway, Switzerland, the UK, and the Western Balkans are drawn from comparable national statistical office releases.

The EU aggregate (32.7 percent) is shown in the ranking table but excluded from the map, which plots national units only. North Macedonia's latest observation is from late 2025; all other figures are early-2026.

Daily-use figures for UK 15–24-year-olds come from the IAB UK survey's age-split microdata. Comparable daily-use splits for other countries are not yet published on a harmonised basis; references to German, Nordic, and Southern European age gradients are drawn from national survey releases cited in the source appendix and are indicative rather than directly comparable.

*This is an editorial framing of a published dataset. Figures are reproduced as cited; the argument about what the shift toward AI implies is the author's.*
