# South Korea Makes the Memory and Builds the Ships. It Just Lost All Three Energy Lines.

*The country that fabricates the majority of the world's AI memory chips and 84% of its LNG carriers is simultaneously losing its oil, its gas, and its helium from the same chokepoint.*

**By vizmaya · March 2026**

---

## $2.49

*Per H100 GPU hour on Lambda Labs*

A startup running 10,000 GPU-hours a month pays $24,900.

 That number is about to move — not because of a chip shortage, but because of a war in a strait most engineers have never thought about. 

This is the pin where that story ends. It starts 12,000 kilometres east.

---

## The crash nobody expected

South Korea's stock market plunged 18% in four trading days. Over $500 billion erased. Samsung and SK Hynix each fell more than 20% in a single week.

The crash is the visible crisis. The market priced the headlines. What it hasn't priced — not yet — is the invisible crisis happening inside the fabs and the shipyards.

---

## 18%

*KOSPI in four trading days*

Worst collapse since 2008. Not a financial crisis. An energy crisis wearing the mask of one.

The crash is noise. This is the signal.

---

## Worst since 2008

In 2008, the crash was global and financial. In 2026, it is local — but the supply chain damage is planetary.

South Korea doesn't just lose market cap. It loses the gas, oil, and helium that make the chips the world's AI infrastructure runs on. The number the market found is real. The number it hasn't found yet is larger.

---

## Act I: The most consequential industrial economy in this crisis

South Korea did not start this war. It has no leverage over the combatants. It does not control the strait.

But it may be the single most important economy in this conflict — because it sits at the intersection of two industries the world cannot function without. Both run on the same energy grid.

---

## Oil: 70%

Seventy percent of South Korea's crude comes from the Middle East. But oil here is not just fuel — it is **naphtha**, the chemical feedstock semiconductor fabs need to make photoresist, solvents, and cleaning agents that build every chip.

At $119 a barrel, 1.7 million barrels stranded daily. The government imposed a one-day-per-week vehicle ban. The first supply line is bleeding.

---

## Gas: 26%

Twenty-six percent of South Korea's electricity comes from LNG. Qatar declared force majeure on Korean contracts the day the strait closed.

SK Hynix had been building a private LNG plant to power its own fabs. That project now carries a three-to-five year disruption notice. The second supply line, severed.

---

## Helium: 64.7%

South Korea sources 64.7% of its helium from Qatar — the highest dependency of any major chip-producing nation on Earth.

Helium is not substitutable in semiconductor manufacturing. It cools plasma etch chambers at temperatures nothing else can reach. The third supply line, the most invisible, is the one that matters most for AI chips.

---

## Icheon: 62% of global HBM

This is the SK Hynix Icheon fab. It holds **62% of global HBM supply** — the memory NVIDIA cannot build an H100, B200, or Blackwell Ultra without.

HBM is sold out through 2026. Zero slack. Every wafer here is already spoken for by Google, Amazon, Microsoft, or Meta. There is no spare capacity anywhere in this supply chain.

---

## Cheongju: the M15X expansion

SK Hynix's M15X fab in Cheongju is the single largest semiconductor investment in Korean history — $15 billion, designed to absorb the HBM4 supercycle.

It runs on the same energy grid. The same helium supply. The same naphtha feedstock. The expansion was built to solve demand pressure. It was not built for this.

---

## Pyeongtaek: Samsung's 33% of global DRAM

Samsung's Pyeongtaek campus holds **33% of global DRAM**. The largest semiconductor complex on Earth by square footage.

When Samsung fell 20% in a single week, this is what the market was pricing — not the balance sheet, but the energy vulnerability of the building behind this pin.

---

## HBM sold out through 2026

NVIDIA derived 27% of SK Hynix's total revenue in H1 2025 — approximately 10.9 trillion Korean won.

UBS projects SK Hynix will capture 70% of the HBM4 market for NVIDIA's Rubin platform. HBM production consumes **3x the wafer capacity** per gigabyte of standard DRAM. Every GPU wafer run is a consumer DRAM run cancelled.

---

## The $54.6 billion supercycle

Bank of America calls 2026 a memory supercycle — $54.6 billion HBM market, up 58% year over year.

This was already the most supply-constrained semiconductor market in history. The hyperscalers were accepting any supply at any price. The strait did not create this scarcity. It inherited a market with no room to absorb any shock at all.

---

## Ulsan: HD Hyundai

HD Hyundai's Ulsan complex is the largest single ship-production site on Earth.

Between 2021 and 2025, Korean yards delivered **248 LNG carriers** to the world. China delivered 48. A single 174,000-cubic-metre carrier costs $220-260 million. Each takes 30-36 months from steel-cutting to delivery. This is not a peripheral industry. It is a pillar of the global energy system.

---

## Geoje: Samsung Heavy and Hanwha Ocean

Samsung Heavy Industries and Hanwha Ocean operate from Geoje Island. Together with HD Hyundai in Ulsan, they hold **two-thirds of the global LNG carrier orderbook** — $71.3 billion in backlog.

These three yards are why the world has the LNG fleet it has today. And all three run on the grid that just lost three supply lines simultaneously.

---

## One grid, two industries, one crisis

Semiconductors and shipbuilding: two critical industries, concentrated in one country, running on one grid.

Renewable energy? **9.6%** of the power mix — less than a third of the global average. South Korea was building *more* LNG power plants to feed the fabs right up until the gas stopped flowing. Oil. Gas. Helium. All from the same chokepoint.

---

## Act II: The chokepoint

The Strait of Hormuz. Twelve miles wide at its narrowest point.

Every energy line South Korea depends on transits here. Oil from the Gulf states. LNG from Qatar's North Field. Helium from Ras Laffan. One closure is a supply disruption. Three simultaneous closures, on three different inputs, is an industrial crisis with no precedent.

---

## Helium Plant 1: offline since March 2

Qatar's Helium 1 plant at Ras Laffan produces **660 million standard cubic feet per year**. Online since 2005. Offline since March 2.

Helium is a byproduct of LNG liquefaction. When the gas stops flowing, helium extraction stops entirely. There is no workaround, no bypass, no alternative process. When gas stops, helium stops.

---

## Helium Plant 2: the world's largest

Helium 2 is the world's single largest helium facility — **1.3 billion standard cubic feet per year**. Online since 2013. Offline since March 2.

Helium 1 and Helium 2 together represent more annual supply than the entire production of any alternative supplier currently operating anywhere on Earth.

---

## Helium Plant 3: all three offline

Helium 3 adds another 400 million scf per year. Qatar's three Ras Laffan plants combined: **33.2% of global helium supply**.

All three offline since March 2. One-third of the world's helium removed in a single week. No region can replace this volume. The countdown has started.

---

## The misleading spike

Spot helium surged 50-100% in March. Northeast Asia hit **$153/MCF** — a 21.5% month-on-month increase. Headlines declared shortage.

Here is what they missed: spot is only **2% of the helium market**. The other 98% trades on long-term contracts at $500-600/MCF. Those contracts have not moved. The invoices are calmer than the headlines. For now.

---

## The 6-month inflection

The real event comes at months four to six. Contracts come up for renewal. Force majeure triggers renegotiation.

If Qatar stays offline, contract prices move from $500-600 toward **$1,000-2,000/MCF** — a 2-4x increase on the 98% of the market that actually matters. That is when Korean fab operating budgets start bleeding. Not yet. The clock is running.

---

## Samsung's buffer: six months

Samsung holds approximately six months of helium stockpile. It runs an **18% consumption reduction** via its Helium Reuse System.

Six months of buffer is not a solution. It is a countdown. The question the market is not asking: what happens at month seven?

---

## TSMC's recycling edge

TSMC maintains helium from multiple suppliers, with over two months of stock. More importantly: **60-75% helium recycling rates**.

Over 70% of fabs in Taiwan and Japan already operate recycling systems. Korean fabs are behind. Their buffer is stockpile. TSMC's buffer is efficiency. When stockpile runs out, that gap becomes a structural cost disadvantage.

---

## The Amarillo mistake

The former US Federal Helium Reserve in Amarillo, Texas — privatised in **June 2024**. One year before the largest helium supply disruption since the 1960s. It can no longer serve as a government strategic buffer.

The US still produces 42% of global helium. It cannot rapidly scale. The insurance policy was sold before anyone knew they'd need to file a claim.

---

## Month 7: no easy solution

Russia's Amur Gas Processing Plant has capacity roughly equal to Qatar's output. It faces Western sanctions. Algeria produces 5-10% of global supply. Tanzania's helium projects are years from production.

If the conflict extends beyond six months, no combination of available alternatives fills the gap. Month seven is a supply arithmetic problem with no good answer.

---

## The transmission chain

Helium scarcity does not just raise costs — it degrades **yield**.

In plasma etch chambers, helium pressure fluctuation causes etch uniformity to fail. The fab produces lower-yield wafers: more wafers required per good chip. That multiplier does not appear on energy bills. It appears in **cost-per-die** — then in DRAM spot price — then in the GPU hour that started this story.

---

## Act III: One chip price tells the whole story

Everyone is asking: will Silicon Valley get its chips?

Wrong question. The right question is: **at what price?**

One number absorbs every upstream shock — the DDR5 16Gb spot price on DRAMeXchange. When Korean energy costs rise, this number moves. When helium contracts reprice, this number moves. Watch this number.

---

## From $6.84 to $27.20

Not a model. Not an assumption. A price that updated every trading day.

From **$6.84** in September 2025 to **$27.20** in December 2025: a 3.4x increase in ten weeks. Before the first missile hit Ras Laffan. The AI boom was already eating its own supply chain.

---

## The pre-existing fire

DRAM prices rose **172%** through 2025. DDR5 contract pricing more than doubled. Samsung halted new DDR5 orders to reassess pricing. SK Hynix reported capacity "essentially sold out" for 2026. Distributors: two to four weeks of inventory.

Hormuz did not create this scarcity. It inherited a market already running at zero slack.

---

## 60-day resolution: +2-4% GPU cost

If conflict resolves in sixty days, stockpiles hold. Helium contracts don't reprice. The Hormuz increment is invisible against the pre-existing supercycle.

DDR5 retreats to ~$18-20. **GPU hour impact: +2-4%.** An H100 hour moves from $2.49 to roughly $2.55-2.59. Absorbed by noise. This is the best case.

---

## 6-month scenario: +12-20% GPU cost

Six months in, helium contracts reprice. Korean fabs cut utilisation 10-30% on helium-critical steps. Energy costs rise 25-35% sustained.

DDR5 climbs to ~$30-35. **GPU hour impact: +12-20%.** An H100 hour reaches ~$2.79-2.99. At 10,000 GPU-hours a month, that is $3,000-5,000 in additional compute cost. Recurring. Every month.

---

## 3-5 year force majeure: +30-50% GPU cost

If force majeure extends to years, the cost curve shifts **permanently**. Korean fabs lose their cost advantage. Micron gains structural edge. CXMT accelerates. The "compute gets cheaper" assumption breaks.

DDR5 floor: $35-40. **GPU hour impact: +30-50%.** An H100 hour at $3.24-3.74. A startup burning 50,000 GPU-hours a month absorbs $450,000 in additional annual compute cost. Permanently.

---

## What +30% means in dollars

At +30%, an H100 hour moves from $2.49 to $3.24. That is $0.75 per hour.

This is not a crisis. It is arithmetic. Every AI business model that assumed compute costs would fall is now being stress-tested — not by a collapse, but by a quiet, structural repricing that started in a strait most engineers have never thought about.

---

## The assumption nobody is stress-testing

Every AI business model runs on one assumption: **compute gets cheaper each generation**. Moore's Law applied to GPU hours.

The memory supercycle was already challenging that assumption before the strait closed. Hormuz — extended past Korea's stockpiles — does not create a crisis. It makes an existing fragility permanent.

---

## Here is the story nobody has told

Everyone covered the chip crisis. The HBM shortage. The memory supercycle.

Nobody covered the shipyards.

South Korea also builds the ships that carry the gas the world needs to replace Qatar's output. And the decisions made in Ulsan and Geoje **this quarter** will determine global LNG fleet capacity in **2028**. The lag is longer than most analysts' forecast horizon.

---

## Act IV: Decisions made now, deliveries missed in 2028

The chip crisis plays out in months. The shipyard crisis plays out in years.

Korean yards hold **$71.3 billion** in LNG carrier backlog. A carrier takes 30-36 months from steel-cutting to delivery. The energy pressure hitting those yards today does not stop the weld. It erodes margin. It makes buyers look at alternatives.

---

## One causal step, thirty-six months of consequence

If a buyer redirects a single LNG carrier order to a Chinese yard in Q2 2026, that ship is not delivered by a Korean yard in Q4 2028.

The world's LNG fleet grows more slowly at exactly the moment it needs maximum capacity to replace Qatar's shortfall. Not a collapse. A **chronic supply squeeze** — the kind that reprices shipping rates quietly, over years.

---

## Japan: the largest buyer

Japan is the world's largest LNG importer by volume. Its regasification terminals at Futtsu depend on regular carrier deliveries from Korean yards.

If Korean yards lose orders to Chinese competitors, Japan's LNG supply chain feels it first at the next contract cycle. Japan is simultaneously trying to source *more* LNG to replace Qatari supply. The carrier fleet is the bottleneck.

---

## China: fastest-growing buyer

China's LNG imports have grown faster than any other nation this decade. CNOOC's Tianjin terminals are expanding capacity.

China is simultaneously the biggest alternative customer for Korean yard orders and the most exposed buyer if Korean yard capacity erodes. Both cause and consequence of the slow bleed — the country most likely to redirect orders, and most harmed when carriers run short.

---

## Europe: rerouting from Russian gas

Since 2022, Europe has been rerouting from Russian pipeline gas to LNG. Rotterdam's Gate Terminal is Europe's largest LNG import facility — built to compensate for what Russia no longer supplies.

Europe's LNG infrastructure was already at capacity. It depends on ships Korean yards are contracted to build, subject to energy-cost pressure the buyers did not model.

---

## USA: Sabine Pass

Sabine Pass in Louisiana is the largest LNG export terminal in the Western hemisphere. Cheniere Energy's fleet is built on Korean-made carriers.

Paradoxically, the US produces 42% of global helium with zero Hormuz exposure — yet depends on the Korean shipyards now under that same pressure. The country least exposed on helium is most dependent on the most exposed yards.

---

## India: the last buyer

India's Mundra LNG terminal serves an economy adding industrial capacity faster than its domestic energy supply can sustain.

Every country on this map is simultaneously trying to source *more* LNG to replace Qatar's output. And the one country that builds the ships to carry it is running on the same broken energy grid. The loop closes here.

---

## The signal to watch now

**Watch Korean yard new-order announcements in Q2-Q3 2026.** A slowdown relative to Chinese yards is the early indicator.

Chinese yards have grown from 15% to 22% of global LNG carrier deliveries since 2023. If Korean energy costs make Korean bids uncompetitive at the margin, that share accelerates — and the slow bleed begins its 36-month countdown.

## The slow bleed

A procurement officer in Seoul watching a helium contract renewal date approach. A shipyard manager in Ulsan receiving fewer bid requests. A startup CFO recalculating compute budget at +20%.

These are not dramatic events. They are not headlines. They are quiet adjustments — people discovering that the invisible infrastructure of the modern economy just got permanently more expensive.

Watch the helium contract price. That is the leading indicator. When it moves, DRAM follows. When DRAM moves, the GPU hour reprices. The full chain, in one sentence.
