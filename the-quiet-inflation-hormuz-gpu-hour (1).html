South Korea Makes the Memory and Builds the Ships. It Just Lost All Three Energy Lines.

The country that produces the majority of the world's AI memory chips and 84% of its LNG carriers is simultaneously losing its oil, its gas, and its helium from the same chokepoint. Here is how that reprices every GPU hour on earth.

by vizzmaya · March 2026
18%
South Korea's stock market plunged 18% in four trading days — the worst since 2008. Over $500 billion erased. Samsung and SK Hynix each fell more than 20% in a single week.
Act I

The most consequential industrial economy in this crisis

South Korea did not start this war. It has no leverage over the combatants. It does not control the strait. But it may be the single most important economy in this conflict — because it sits at the intersection of two industries the world cannot function without, and both are powered by the same energy grid.

The government imposed fuel rationing on March 25: a one-day-per-week vehicle ban for 1.5 million government vehicles, enforced by licence plate number. Oil at $119 a barrel. 1.7 million barrels of crude bound for Seoul delayed or stranded every day.

But the fuel rationing is the visible crisis. The invisible one is what's happening inside the fabs and the shipyards.

The memory dominance

SK Hynix holds 62% of global HBM — the memory NVIDIA cannot build an H100, B200, or Blackwell Ultra without. Samsung holds 33% of global DRAM. Combined: the majority of memory in every AI training cluster on earth. HBM is sold out through 2026. Zero slack.

The shipbuilding dominance

Here's the connection nobody else has made. South Korea also builds 84% of the world's LNG carriers. HD Hyundai, Samsung Heavy, and Hanwha Ocean delivered 248 carriers since 2021 — versus 48 from China. Korean yards hold two-thirds of the global orderbook at $71.3 billion. A single carrier costs $220-260 million and takes 30-36 months to build.

One grid, all exposed

Both industries — semiconductors and shipbuilding — run on the same energy grid. A grid where fossil fuels dominate. Where renewable energy is just 9.6% of the power mix — less than a third of the global average. Where the country was building more LNG power plants to feed the fabs, right up until the gas stopped flowing.

Act II

The chokepoint: how Qatar's helium reaches a Korean fab

The chain that connects the Strait of Hormuz to a GPU shortage in Santa Clara runs through helium extraction plants, cryogenic separation, and a market structure that almost nobody covering this story has gotten right.

Qatar's three helium plants at Ras Laffan produce roughly one-third of global supply. All three have been offline since March 2, because helium is extracted from the natural gas stream during liquefaction. When the gas stops flowing, the helium stops flowing. One-third of global supply, removed in a single week.

But the helium market has a structure that makes the impact slower than the headlines suggest — and more dangerous than the reassurances admit.

Helium price: spot vs. contract ($/MCF)
Sources: IMARC, ChemAnalyst, Phil Kornbluth (CNBC). *Projected if crisis extends.
The misleading spike

Spot helium surged 50-100% in March. NE Asia hit $153/MCF. Headlines screamed shortage. But here's what they didn't say: spot is only 2% of the helium market. The other 98% trades on long-term contracts at $500-600/MCF. Those contracts haven't moved. The invoices are calmer than the headlines.

The 6-month inflection

The real event comes at month 4-6. Contracts come up for renewal. Force majeure triggers renegotiation. If Qatar stays offline, contract prices climb toward $1,000-2,000/MCF — a 2-4x increase on the 98% of the market that actually matters. That's when Korean fabs feel it in their operating budgets.

The stockpile runway

Samsung holds ~6 months of helium stockpile plus an 18% consumption reduction via its Helium Reuse System. TSMC has 2+ months from diversified suppliers. Over 70% of Taiwan/Japan fabs run closed-loop recycling. The question isn't whether there's a buffer — there is. The question is what happens at month 7.

Act III

The feedback loop nobody has connected

South Korea makes the chips. That's the story everyone has told. Here's the story nobody has told.

South Korea also builds the ships that carry the gas that the rest of the world needs to replace Qatar's output.

The same economy. The same energy grid. The same chokepoint.

The doom loop: energy crisis ↔ carrier capacity ↔ deeper energy crisis
Feedback loop model. Circuit breakers shown in green. Positive feedback in red.
The loop

Korea's energy crisis puts pressure on the shipyards that build the LNG carriers the world needs. If carrier construction slips, the global LNG fleet grows more slowly. If there aren't enough ships, the gas shortage deepens. If the shortage deepens, Korean industry takes a harder hit. The loop tightens.

The circuit breakers

The doom loop has brakes. Korea is restarting 5 nuclear reactors and easing coal restrictions. Shipbuilding is less energy-intensive than chipmaking. ~60 idle LNG carriers provide buffer. Any shipyard disruption only affects 2028-2029 deliveries. The loop grinds — it doesn't spiral immediately. But the longer the war lasts, the deeper it cuts.

Act IV

How this reprices a GPU hour in Virginia

Everyone is asking: will Silicon Valley get its chips?

Wrong question. The right question is: at what price?

The answer depends on something most AI companies haven't noticed: the memory crisis was already the biggest cost driver before Hormuz.

172%
DRAM prices rose 172% through 2025. DDR5 contract pricing more than doubled. Server DRAM climbed 60% quarter-over-quarter in Q1 2026. All before the strait closed.

The pre-existing fire

HBM production consumes 3x the wafer capacity per gigabyte vs. standard DRAM. Every wafer allocated to an NVIDIA GPU is a wafer denied to smartphones, laptops, servers. Google, Amazon, Microsoft, Meta placed open-ended orders — accepting any supply at any cost. The AI boom was eating its own supply chain.

60 days: a rounding error

At 60-day resolution, Korean stockpiles hold. Contracts don't reprice. The Hormuz increment on DRAM is +2-4% — invisible against the 172% supercycle. The crisis hitting compute costs has nothing to do with the strait. Not yet.

6 months: visible

At six months, helium contracts reprice. Korean fabs face utilisation cuts. Energy costs rise 25-35% sustained. The South Korea feedback loop tightens. The Hormuz increment climbs to +15-25%. Now it shows on the chart.

3-5 years: structural reset

The cost curve shifts permanently. Korean fabs lose their cost advantage to Micron (US-based, no Hormuz exposure). Chinese memory makers accelerate. Korean shipyard dominance erodes. The competitive map reorganises around energy security, not just technology.

The assumption nobody is stress-testing
Every AI business model assumes compute gets cheaper. The memory supercycle challenged that assumption. Hormuz — if it extends past South Korea's stockpiles — breaks it.
What to watch

For procurement

Accelerate helium recycling investment now. Monitor helium contract renewal dates — the repricing signal comes before the shortage. Naphtha and petrochemical input costs are already rising double digits.

For investors

60-day scenario is the base case. Start discounting at month 4-5 when helium contracts reprice. Watch Linde and Air Products as lead indicators. Watch Korean shipyard order cancellations for the structural signal.

For AI builders

Model unit economics with compute costs flat or +20-30% for 18 months. If your model breaks at +30% GPU cost, it was already fragile. The memory supercycle was going to test you before Hormuz did.
