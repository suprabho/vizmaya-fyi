South Korea Makes the Memory and Builds the Ships. It Just Lost All Three Energy Lines.

The country that makes the majority of the world's AI memory chips and 84% of its LNG carriers is losing its oil, its gas, and its helium through the same chokepoint at the same time. This is what that does to the cost of running an AI model.

Act I

The most consequential industrial economy in this crisis

South Korea did not start this war. It has no leverage over the combatants and no control over the strait. But it may be the most exposed economy in this conflict, because it sits at the intersection of two industries nobody has figured out how to replace, and both run on the same energy grid.

The government imposed fuel rationing on March 25: a one-day-per-week vehicle ban for 1.5 million government vehicles, enforced by licence plate number. Oil at $119 a barrel. 1.7 million barrels of crude bound for Seoul delayed or stranded every day.

That is the visible crisis. What is happening inside the fabs and the shipyards is quieter, and considerably worse.

South Korea's triple exposure: three supply lines, one chokepoint

Oil exposure

Oil: 70% of crude from the Middle East. Used for naphtha, a feedstock for semiconductor chemicals, not just fuel. Now on the government's "economic security" watchlist. Oil at $119 a barrel.

Helium exposure

Helium: 64.7% from Qatar. The highest dependency of any major chip-producing nation. No substitute exists for plasma etch cooling. One-third of global supply removed in a single week.

Gas (LNG) exposure

Gas (LNG): 26% of electricity generated from gas. Qatar declared force majeure on Korean contracts. SK Hynix was building an LNG plant to power its own fabs. That gas now carries a 3-5 year disruption notice.

Global HBM market: South Korea holds 90% of supply; every AI GPU depends on it
Sources: Counterpoint Research (HBM Q2 2025), TrendForce, IDC. DRAM shares are 2026 estimates.
The memory dominance

SK Hynix holds 62% of global HBM, the memory type NVIDIA cannot build an H100, B200, or Blackwell Ultra without. Samsung holds 33% of global DRAM. Combined, they account for the majority of memory in every AI training cluster on earth. HBM is sold out through 2026. Zero slack.

One grid, all exposed

Both industries run on the same energy grid, one where fossil fuels dominate. Renewable energy covers just 9.6% of the power mix, less than a third of the global average. The country was building more LNG power plants to feed the fabs, right up until the gas stopped flowing.

South Korea builds 84% of the world's LNG carriers
Sources: BusinessKorea (248 vs 48 carrier deliveries, 2021-2025), VesselsValue ($71.3B orderbook).
The shipbuilding dominance

South Korea also builds 84% of the world's LNG carriers. HD Hyundai, Samsung Heavy Industries, and Hanwha Ocean delivered 248 carriers between 2021 and 2025, versus 48 from China. Korean yards hold two-thirds of the global orderbook at $71.3 billion in the LNG segment alone.

The orderbook

A single 174,000-cubic-metre LNG carrier costs $250-270 million and takes 30-36 months from steel cutting to delivery. Gas carriers make up over 60% of Korean shipyard order composition. Korean vessel exports hit $31.8 billion in 2025. This is not a peripheral industry. It is a pillar of the economy.

Act II

The chokepoint: how Qatar's helium reaches a Korean fab

The connection between the Strait of Hormuz and a GPU shortage in Santa Clara runs through helium extraction plants, cryogenic separation, and a market structure that most coverage of this story has gotten completely wrong.

Qatar's three helium plants at Ras Laffan produce roughly one-third of global supply: 33.2%, according to the USGS 2026 Mineral Commodity Summaries. All three have been offline since March 2. When the gas stops flowing, the helium stops flowing. One-third of global supply, gone in a single week.

Ras Laffan, Qatar: three helium plants, 33% of global supply
Sources: USGS 2026 Mineral Commodity Summaries (33.2% of global supply). Plant data: Gasworld, Helium One.
Three plants at Ras Laffan

Qatar's three helium plants produce 33.2% of global supply. Helium 1 (660M scf/yr, online 2005), Helium 2 (1.3B scf/yr, the world's largest, online 2013), and Helium 3 (400M scf/yr, online 2021). Combined: 2,360M scf/yr.

All offline since March 2

All three plants have been offline since March 2. Helium is extracted from the natural gas stream during cryogenic liquefaction. When the gas stops flowing, the helium stops flowing. One-third of global supply, removed in a single week.

No easy replacement

The United States produces 42% of global helium but cannot rapidly scale. Russia's Amur plant faces Western sanctions. Algeria is only 5-10% of supply. Tanzania is years from production. There is no easy replacement.

March 2026: Spot at $153/MCF, but only 2% of the market. Contracts unmoved.
Sources: IMARC (NE Asia Mar 2026: $152.70/MCF +21.5% MoM), ChemAnalyst, Phil Kornbluth (CNBC). *Projected.
The misleading spike

Spot helium surged 50-100% in March. Northeast Asia hit $153/MCF. Headlines screamed shortage. But spot is only 2% of the helium market. The other 98% trades on long-term contracts at $500-600/MCF delivered. Those contracts have not moved. Not yet.

The 6-month inflection

The real event comes at months 4-6. Contracts come up for renewal. Force majeure triggers renegotiation. If Qatar stays offline, contract prices climb toward $1,000-2,000/MCF, a 2-4x increase on the 98% of the market that actually matters.

The stockpile runway

Samsung's supply chain holds roughly 6 months of helium at the logistics level, plus an 18% consumption reduction from its Helium Reuse System. Working fab inventory is closer to six weeks. TSMC draws from diversified suppliers with 80-90% recycling rates and 2+ months of cover. The buffer is real. The question is what happens when it runs out at month 7.

Act III

One chip price tells the whole story

Everyone is asking whether Silicon Valley will get its chips. That is the wrong question. The right question is: at what price?

The answer is visible in a single data point tracked daily by DRAMeXchange: the spot price of a DDR5 16Gb chip.

The pre-existing fire

DRAM prices rose 172% through 2025. Server DRAM climbed ~90% QoQ in Q1 2026, the largest quarterly increase on record. HBM consumes 3× the wafer capacity per gigabyte. Every wafer for an NVIDIA GPU is a wafer denied to everything else. The AI boom was eating its own supply chain.

The supercycle origin

Google, Amazon, Microsoft, and Meta placed open-ended orders, accepting any supply at any cost. The DDR5 16Gb spot price absorbs every upstream shock into one number. The memory supercycle was already the crisis. Hormuz is the accelerant.

60-day resolution: +2-4% GPU hour

If the conflict resolves within 60 days, stockpiles hold. Helium contracts don't reprice. Spot retreats to ~$18-20. The Hormuz increment is invisible against the 172% pre-existing surge. GPU hour impact: +2-4% from baseline.

6-month scenario: +12-20% GPU hour

Helium contracts reprice. The 98% starts moving toward $1,000-1,500/MCF. Korean fabs cut utilisation 10-30% on helium-critical steps. Energy costs up 25-35% sustained. DDR5 climbs to ~$30-35. GPU hour impact: +12-20%.

3-5 year force majeure: +30-50% GPU hour

The cost curve shifts permanently. Korean fabs lose their cost advantage. Micron, US-based with zero Hormuz exposure, gains structural edge. CXMT accelerates its climb. DDR5 stabilises at a $35-40 structural floor. GPU hour impact: +30-50%. The assumption that compute always gets cheaper stops being an assumption and starts being a question.

Act IV

The slow bleed: decisions made now, deliveries missed in 2028

South Korea makes the chips. That part of the story has been told. Here is the part that has not been told.

South Korea also builds 84% of the world's LNG carriers. HD Hyundai, Samsung Heavy, and Hanwha Ocean hold a $71.3B LNG-specific backlog. Every carrier takes 30-36 months from order to delivery.

The obvious objection here is that shipyard order books are sticky. Customers do not cancel $260M vessels lightly. That is true. But orders at the margin do migrate when Korean yards become uncompetitive on cost. If rising energy costs compress Korean margins in 2026, some percentage of new orders go to Chinese yards instead. Those are ships that will not exist in 2028. One causal step. Thirty-six months of consequence.

Korean yards: the world's LNG shipbuilder

HD Hyundai (Ulsan), Samsung Heavy, and Hanwha Ocean (Geoje) export LNG carriers to Japan, China, Europe, Qatar, the USA, India, Malaysia, and Australia. They build for the customers of the gas they can no longer afford. The $71.3B LNG backlog is the world's liquefied gas logistics buffer, and it runs on Korean energy.

The signal to watch now

Track Q2-Q3 2026 order announcements from HD Hyundai and Samsung Heavy. If Chinese yards (CSSC, DSIC) gain LNG carrier share, the 2028 fleet gap is confirmed before a single delivery is missed. Every order that migrates now is a ship the world will not have in 2028-2029, when alternative LNG supply needs to move.

What to watch

For procurement

Accelerate helium recycling investment now, before contracts reprice. The repricing signal comes before the shortage, not after. Naphtha and petrochemical input costs are already up double digits. That is not a projection.

For investors

The 60-day scenario is the base case. Start building in downside at month 4-5, when helium contracts come up for renewal. Watch Linde and Air Products as lead indicators. Korean shipyard order cancellations are the signal for the structural scenario, not the market.

For AI builders

Model your unit economics with compute costs flat or up 20-30% for 18 months. If your model breaks at +30% GPU cost, it was already fragile. The memory supercycle was going to test you before Hormuz did. This just moves the timeline forward.
